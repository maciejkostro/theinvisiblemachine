# Speed, Scale, and Price of Convenience

Payments are our most frequent point of contact with the financial system. We treat them routinely and usually notice them only when something does not work.

Published: 2026-04-03

## What this essay is about

This essay turns to retail payments as the most routine point of contact between users and the financial system. It looks beyond the familiarity of card use and asks what kind of infrastructure, pricing logic, and institutional dependence make this everyday convenience possible.

## Why it matters

Payments are usually noticed only when they fail. That makes them easy to treat as neutral background. The essay shows that their apparent simplicity rests on a concentrated structure of schemes, fees, processing rules, and external dependencies that matter far beyond the point of sale.

Payments are our most frequent point of contact with the financial system. It is impossible to overstate the effect of smoothly functioning payments on the economy. They also shape our expectations regarding the system’s dependability and certainty.

Despite considerable standardisation at the global level for cards and the European level for interbank transfers, payments remain closely tied to the traditions of individual countries and, in some cases, to local schemes. But regardless of that, payment cards remain the most important instrument.

According to statistics from the European Central Bank, cards accounted for more than half of all cashless transactions in the euro area, and this share was rising despite the rise in instant account-to-account payments. This dominant position is particularly visible in payments at physical points of sale.

In many European countries, local schemes are of major importance to the payments market. A few examples:

- **iDEAL (WERO)** in the Netherlands, used above all for e-commerce payments and account-to-account transfers
- **BLIK** in Poland, used for mobile payments, transfers to a phone number, e-commerce, and point-of-sale payments
- **Swish** in Sweden, widely used for P2P transfers and mobile payments
- **Bizum** in Spain, available in the apps of most Spanish banks and used for e-commerce payments and P2P transfers
- **Girocard** in Germany, still an important payment instrument for local in-store payments

The schemes described above create an impression of diversity and resilience in individual national markets. Foreign payments, by contrast, are dominated by international card schemes.

This constant dependence causes long-term effects. Bank transfers are higher in value, mainly because of corporate and interbank payments. Card payments dominate in terms of number and frequency. Frequent low-value card payments shape consumer behaviour and have a direct effect on merchants. In this segment, Visa and Mastercard act as gatekeepers for European commerce.

This role follows from several aspects. These companies define the rules of cooperation and the technical standards. They set the price structure and dispute-resolution procedures. They control access to transaction data and how that data is used. Dependence on an external legal system is written into the rules of operation of the schemes because both Visa and Mastercard are American companies.

In 2023, these two schemes processed transactions in Europe worth many trillions of euros, covering almost all European retail points of sale. Participation in a scheme is formally voluntary. In practice, withdrawal means excluding merchants and consumers from everyday economic activity.

The technical foundation of this system is the so-called four-party model. Every card transaction involves the cardholder, the merchant, the issuing bank, and the merchant’s bank. At the scheme level, a protocol governs authorisation, clearing, and settlement.

This system, which works well and consistently, is also highly centralised.

Fees in the card ecosystem are complex. The interchange fee compensates the issuing bank for programme costs and exposure to risk. Scheme participation fees finance operational activities and management. The acquirer’s margin covers processing and services for merchants. EU Regulation 2015/751 imposed limits on interchange fees for consumer debit and credit cards. These limits do not apply to commercial cards or to scheme participation fees.

The regulation did not lead to a decline in overall merchant costs. The fee structure changed. Scheme participation fees generated additional categories, differentiated by merchant type and transaction channel. Regulatory costs were redistributed and ultimately borne by merchants.

For consumers, these costs are usually invisible. For merchants, they are unavoidable.

Apart from fees, card payments generate a large volume of transaction data. Every record for a single transaction contains information about location, time, merchant category, device type, and more. Aggregate data at the continental level gives a very precise picture of economic activity. Most of these data are managed by non-European entities and are the subject of non-European law.

European banks bear the weight of risk and regulatory requirements. They may also be exposed to the greatest economic risk, especially in crisis situations.

The suspension of Visa and Mastercard operations in Russia in 2022 illustrates geopolitical pressure. The local payments market did not collapse only because a national card payment operator had already been created and transaction processing was switched over. This is not an example of default resilience; it is proof of the importance of earlier decisions regarding system architecture.

Europe does not have a single alternative system that would allow independent processing of card transactions. Some member states maintain local infrastructure; others do not.

Mobile wallets add another level of complication. Services such as Apple Pay and Google Pay do not replace card schemes; they merely change their interface. Tokenisation improves security but also gives platforms control over access and over adding and removing tokenised cards. If the token is inactive, the card is theoretically valid, but payment becomes impossible for users who rely only on smartphones.

European antitrust authorities intervened in certain areas, including access to the NFC interface. These actions improved competitiveness somewhat, but did not change the actual dependence on non-European operating systems and the hardware ecosystem.

Card infrastructure is so popular because it works. It offers predictability and certainty of transaction. That is why customers and merchants trust it, and why it is so difficult to replace it.

Europe should ask itself whether dependence on a narrow group of non-European infrastructure providers is still acceptable for a service as fundamental as retail payments.
