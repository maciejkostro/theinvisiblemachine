# Wallets as Points of Control

In common understanding, a digital wallet is the electronic equivalent of a physical wallet, a place where payment cards are stored. From the system’s point of view, this is not a strict description.

Published: 2026-04-03

## What this essay is about

This essay argues that digital wallets are not simply places where payment instruments are stored. They form an additional layer between the user and the underlying payment system, shaping default choices, tokenisation, authentication, and access to the interface through which the payment is initiated.

## Why it matters

Wallets do not replace banks, accounts, or card schemes. They stand in front of them. That position gives platform and device providers a strong role in determining how payment credentials are used and under what technical conditions transactions can proceed. The result is a new concentration of practical control at the level of the interface.

In common understanding, a digital wallet is the electronic equivalent of a physical wallet. Indeed, early wallets served as simple places to store payment instruments and the authentication data associated with them.

As wallets developed, however, their role grew and came to consist more in coordinating which instrument is used by default and which authentication data are presented. This distinction matters because it affects customer behaviour and risk.

Ease of use and the widespread availability of some devices led some payment instruments to be used more often than others. The default application and the default card in the wallet will be tapped against the terminal more often than anything else that requires the user to take an extra action.

Wallets remodelled intermediation without replacing anything. They sit between the user and the payment infrastructure; they determine the instrument, manage authentication and authorisation, and aggregate data. From the user’s point of view, they are part of the payment system. From the system’s point of view, however, they became powerful tools of control.

This is particularly clear in the example of wallets offered by large platforms. Apple Pay and Google Pay do not issue cards, maintain accounts, offer credit, or settle transactions. All of this remains the domain of banks and card schemes. They do, however, control card tokenisation processes, authentication and authorisation methods, and access to the interface.

This creates a very complex regulatory picture. Banks remain responsible for complying with prudential, conduct, and consumer protection rules. Schemes take on transaction processing and dispute resolution. Platforms control access to the interface and the technical conditions for initiating the transaction.

Theoretically, responsibility is divided among actors, but unevenly. Tokenisation illustrates this unevenness. The tokenisation process centralised control over the life cycle of credentials. Tokens can be suspended, reissued, or blocked without the active participation of the issuing bank. As a rule, this is motivated by security considerations. Central banks and neutral sources, however, point to the shift in governance and dependence.

Another problem is access to NFC modules on end devices. For years, providers of mobile operating systems limited NFC access exclusively to their own wallets. In 2024, the European Commission concluded antitrust proceedings against Apple regarding third-party wallet access to the NFC module on devices running iOS and ordered that this access be opened.

This decision improved the situation of other providers somewhat, but it did not cause independence from the operating system, certification, and the rules defined by platforms.

These market conditions prompted banks to reconsider creating their own wallets. Such initiatives appeared in different parts of Europe, adopted different strategies, but shared one common denominator: reducing dependence on external providers without losing usefulness to customers.

BLIK represents an initiative fully driven by banks and firmly anchored in local payment infrastructure. Vipps is a model managed by a consortium and based on bank ownership, shared infrastructure, and a common brand. WERO, developed by EPI, is the leading pan-European initiative that directly addresses the need to free the European payment system from dependence on card schemes and platform-run wallets.

These three initiatives show the different scales of the problem. The local BLIK has difficulties with cross-border scale. A regional banking consortium must balance the diverse interests of its members. The European initiative must take into account complex coordination and slow adoption.

What they have in common is the awareness that the wallet is not a neutral container for cards. The wallet is a very important layer of governance, and whoever controls orchestration at this level has a real effect on which payment instruments are used, how identity is confirmed, and where risk is located.

Regulatory interventions reduce market inequalities to some extent. The dependence, however, still exists. Platform wallets concentrate control over user behaviour, data access, and the technical aspects of payment initiation. Regulators do not yet know how to address this well in legislation.

## What follows from this

Once the wallet is seen as a point of control rather than a neutral container, the broader issue comes into view: digital finance is not shaped only by regulated institutions, but also by the actors who organise access, defaults, and the technical conditions of participation.
